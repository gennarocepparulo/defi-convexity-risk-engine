# Convexity, Impermanent Loss, and Martingale Structure in Uniswap v3

This note formalizes the economic and probabilistic structure underlying Uniswap v3 LP strategies.

We show that liquidity provision can be interpreted as **exposure to concavity (short gamma)**, and that optimal strategy design corresponds to **controlling convexity through range selection and rebalancing**.

---

## 1. Price Process and Filtration

Let the underlying price follow a Geometric Brownian Motion:

\[
dS_t = \mu S_t dt + \sigma S_t dW_t
\]

Under standard assumptions, the **discounted price process** is a martingale:

\[
\mathbb{E}[S_{t+1} \mid \mathcal{F}_t] = S_t
\]

where \( \mathcal{F}_t \) is the natural filtration generated by the price process.

---

## 2. LP Payoff as a Concave Function

A Uniswap v3 LP position behaves locally like a **constant-product AMM** within its active range.

The LP portfolio value can be approximated as a **concave function of price**:

- Gains are limited when price trends
- Losses accumulate when price moves away and returns

This implies:

- LP is **short convexity (short gamma)**
- HODL is **linear in price**

---

## 3. Impermanent Loss as Convexity Drag

Define relative performance:

\[
X_t = V^{LP}_t - V^{HODL}_t
\]

Under:
- zero fees  
- no rebalancing  

we obtain:

\[
\mathbb{E}[X_{t+1} \mid \mathcal{F}_t] \leq X_t
\]

This means:

> **LP relative performance is a supermartingale**

### Interpretation

- Concavity + martingale price ⇒ negative expected drift
- This is the formal expression of **impermanent loss**

---

## 4. Fees as a Drift Term

When fees are introduced:

\[
X_t = \underbrace{\text{Concavity Drag}}_{\text{negative}} + \underbrace{\text{Fees}}_{\text{positive}}
\]

Fees depend on:

- trading volume (∝ volatility)
- time spent in range
- liquidity concentration (inverse of width)

Thus:

> Fees act as a **volatility-linked positive drift**

---

## 5. Rebalancing as a Stopping-Time Mechanism

In practice, LP strategies include **rebalancing rules**, such as:

- exit when price leaves the range
- periodic recentering

Each rebalance:

- realizes accumulated IL
- resets the reference price
- starts a new "segment"

This creates a **piecewise process**:

\[
X_t = \sum_{k} X^{(k)}_t
\]

where each segment begins at a stopping time.

---

## 6. From Supermartingale to Controlled Process

With fees and rebalancing:

- The process is no longer a pure supermartingale
- It becomes a **controlled stochastic process**

Controls include:

- range width (controls curvature)
- rebalancing rule (controls stopping times)
- cost parameters (frictions)

---

## 7. Strategy as Convexity Control

We can reinterpret LP strategy design as:

> **Choosing controls such that the conditional drift is non-negative**

Formally:

\[
\mathbb{E}[X_{t+1} - X_t \mid \mathcal{F}_t] \geq 0
\]

This requires balancing:

### Trade-off

| Component | Effect |
|----------|--------|
| Narrow range | ↑ fees, ↑ rebalances, ↑ IL realization |
| Wide range | ↓ fees, ↓ activity, ↓ convexity |
| Rebalancing | resets IL, incurs costs |

---

## 8. Connection to Simulation Results

Monte Carlo results show:

- Optimal width lies in an **interior band (~15–25%)**
- Too narrow → excessive convexity drag + costs  
- Too wide → insufficient fee generation  

This is consistent with:

> Optimal strategies **neutralize convexity drag** via fee income and controlled resets

---

## 9. Key Insight

> Uniswap v3 LP is not a passive yield strategy, but a **short convexity position with dynamic control**.

Profitability arises when:

- volatility is high enough (fee generation)
- rebalancing prevents large divergence
- costs remain contained

---

## 10. Summary

- LP payoff is **concave → supermartingale (no fees)**
- Fees introduce **positive drift**
- Rebalancing creates a **segmented stochastic process**
- Strategy = **convexity + stopping-time control**

---

## 11. Implications

- LPs are effectively **selling convexity**
- Fees are compensation for **providing liquidity under uncertainty**
- Optimal design resembles **gamma trading under costs**

---

## Volatility Harvesting vs Convexity Drag

A central result of the project is that LPing in AMMs should be understood as a volatility-harvesting strategy with structural convexity costs.
Fee income tends to be generated gradually and frequently. In most simulated paths, LP earns small gains from trading flow while remaining near the center of the active range. This creates the intuitive appearance that liquidity provision is profitable “most of the time.”
However, this view is incomplete. The same AMM mechanics that generate fee income also impose a concave payoff structure. When prices move substantially away from the reference level, the LP position realizes large convexity losses relative to HODL. These losses are infrequent, but severe. As a result, the distribution of terminal LP − HODL outcomes is highly asymmetric: many small gains are offset by a small number of very large losses.
This tension defines the economics of AMM LPing:

Fees reward local activity and support a high win rate.
Convexity drag penalizes large excursions and generates tail losses.
Transaction costs amplify the damage when the strategy responds too aggressively.

The empirical evidence confirms this interpretation. Even under the best-performing policy found in the simulations, LP outperforms HODL in a majority of paths, yet the average LP − HODL remains negative. This is the characteristic signature of a short-volatility / short-gamma strategy: frequent small gains, rare dominant losses.
In this sense, volatility has a dual effect. It increases fee generation, but it also accelerates convexity realization through larger price moves and more frequent stopping times. The central problem of AMM design is therefore not how to “capture volatility” in isolation, but how to determine whether fee income is sufficient to compensate for convexity drag and implementation costs.
